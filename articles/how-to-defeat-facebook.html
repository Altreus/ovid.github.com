How to Defeat Facebook

Let's see if we can figure this out. Given that Facebook has 2.3 billion monthly active users1, millions of whom wanting anything else, and with 15 million of their most valuable customers walking away since 20172, but still maintaining almost seven billion dollars in net income for the final quarter of 20183, you would think that investors would be all over funding competitors. Taking a percentage of Facebook's market share is a huge business opportunity, but investors don't seem to be biting. Why is that? And can it be changed?

Well, as it turns out, many investors have been biting. They've also been bitten. With seeing so many other general purpose social networks getting up, swinging, and missing, it's no wonder that investors are wary. However, I argue that if you look closer, you can see that Facebook has a fatal weakness that it is struggling to overcome and the contenders have all (sometimes quite hilariously) ignored it.

The Background

Many would-be competitors have tried to take on Facebook, with the recent Google Plus failure being one of the more dramatic failures. Given the long history, we need a lot of backstory to unpack what's going on here.

First, let's distinguish between a General Purpose Social Network (GPSN) and a Special Purpose Social Network (SPSN) (though there's a lot of grey area here). Many of the millions leaving Facebook for Instagram4, are leaving a GPSN for a SPSN. Instagram is for sharing photos and videos and that's it. If you have no photos or videos to share, you may not find an Instagram account as appealing, though you may have one to follow others. Twitter is closer to a SPSN, but it's still rather limited in its features (that, in itself, is a feature). Facebook, like Myspace, or the now-defunct Google Plus, is a GPSN. It has a wide variety of features that appeal to many people. Want to connect but don't want to share photos? Facebook. Want to connect and don't want to be limited to 280 characters? Facebook. Want to connect and regularly organize or participate in local events? Again, Facebook.

GPSNs are more geared towards general-purpose social networking without trying to shoehorn you into a particular mode of sharing. While many people enjoy a variety of social networks, Facebook dominates in in terms of daily user engagement5, with half of its users logging on more than once a day.

The distinction between GPSN and SPSN is important because we're only focusing on the former. The latter gets lots of traction in narrower markets and has a robust, lively community of competitors. GPSN are pretty much "Facebook or nothing" as of this writing.
