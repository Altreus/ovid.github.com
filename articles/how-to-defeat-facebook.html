Disclaimer

You'd pretty much have to be locked in a shed for a couple of decades to not know of the problems we currently have with social networking. They're fairly well-known and discussed, but the "solutions" put forward have been laughably inadequate. Sadly, as I've discovered from two decades of writing online (god, I feel old), I've found that I get much better response from short posts than from long posts (hello, Twitter!). That means that complicated topics can't be easily covered in a way that people will read. Social networking is a complicated topic, but it's also incredibly important. Even if you disagree with my assessment, please keep reading. This problem needs to be solved. If I'm an idiot, please tell me in the comments below.

Social Networking is Broken

Let's be honest: social networks are broken. They’re driving us apart instead of bringing us together. You hop onto Facebook and see old friends from school spewing vile accusations about your favorite political party. Your beloved uncle is sharing another stupid meme while you sigh and search Snopes for a URL to post in response. You see close friends in relationships publicly, bitterly, "break up" online where everyone can read their accusations. And that's the mild stuff; the hard stuff is much, much worse.

You jump from news stories about Facebook being used to build support for genocide1 to Facebook being used for propaganda campaigns to manipulate voters2 to Facebook livestreaming a mass murder in New Zealand3.

And it's not stopping, though Facebook is belatedly responding. They take down the livestreams of the regular rapes4, the gang rapes5, the child rapes6, the murders and suicides7, and more. This is somewhat better than in 2016 when Facebook refused to remove the livestream of a murder because "it doesn’t violate the company’s community standards"8.

And before we go any further, just ask yourself one question: how can a company get so wrong that they think streaming murders for entertainment isn't violating "community standards"?

The Opportunity

Let's see if we can figure this out. Facebook has 2.3 billion monthly active users9, millions of whom want anything else, and 15 million of their most valuable customers have walked away since 201710. Despite this, Facebook still basked in almost seven billion dollars in net income for the final quarter of 201811. You would think that investors would be drooling at this. Millions of customers begging for a new market! Even more interesingly, there's almost no barrier to entry into this market. Taking a percentage of Facebook's market share is a huge business opportunity but investors don't seem to be biting. Why is that? And can it be changed?

Well, as it turns out, investors have been biting. They've also been bitten. With seeing so many other social networks getting up, swinging, and missing, it's no wonder that investors are wary. However, once you start diving into mess, you find that many otherwise savvy investors have fallen for tulip mania12 and haven't paid attention to the real issues customers care about. Thus, the contenders fail.

The Background

Many of these would-be competitors have tried to take on Facebook, with the recent Google Plus shutdown being one of the more dramatic failures. Given the long history, we need a lot of backstory to unpack what's going on here.

First, let's distinguish between a General Purpose Social Network (GPSN) and a Special Purpose Social Network (SPSN) (though there's a lot of grey area here). Many of the millions leaving Facebook for Instagram13, are leaving a GPSN for a SPSN. Instagram is for sharing photos and videos. If you have no photos or videos to share, you may not find an Instagram account as appealing, though you may have one to follow others. Twitter is closer to a GPSN, but it's still rather limited in its features (that, in itself, is a feature). Facebook, like Myspace, or the now-defunct Google Plus, is a GPSN. It has a wide variety of features that appeal to many people. Want to connect but don't want to share photos? Facebook. Want to connect and don't want to be limited to 280 characters? Facebook. Want to connect and regularly organize or participate in local events? Again, Facebook.

GPSNs are more geared towards general-purpose social networking without trying to shoehorn you into a particular mode of sharing. While many people enjoy a variety of social networks, Facebook dominates in in terms of daily user engagement14, with half of its users logging on more than once a day.

The distinction between GPSN and SPSN is important because this article is only focusing on the former. The latter gets lots of traction in narrower markets and has a robust, lively community of competitors. GPSNs are pretty much "Facebook or nothing" as of this writing.
