I'll cut right to the chase so you can decide if this worth reading or not.

I'm an American who's lived in Europe (multiple countries) since 2006. Here in Europe, in debating what citizenship means, we have a lot of discussion about our rights and responsibilities.

In the US, we have a lot of discussion about our rights.

That subtle difference changes everything.

When I first moved to the UK back in 2006, many British people asked me if I moved over for political reasons. I responded with three word “Vice President Blair.” It didn't matter their political views, they invariably laughed. Without going into the background of that joke, suffice it to say that it defused a possibly tense conversation about politics. I had moved to Europe for adventure, not as some grand political gesture.

Here I am, in quarantine in 2020 due to the coronavirus. I'm living in a house in the south of France, just north of Cannes. I've spent several years in the UK, just over a year in the Netherlands, and about eight years in France. I've friends from all over the world—especially Europe—and I've finally come to an understanding of what civil society means to Europeans. And with that understanding, as I watch armed Americans protesting on the steps of capital buildings, demanding—at gunpoint—the right be exposed to a lethal virus, I've finally come to understand what is broken with the United States.

I was born in the US. I was raised in the US. I was a military brat and I still love my country. This is not a blog post saying that America sucks or that it's a failed state. There are still many wonderful things about the US, but it's broken. It's been broken for a long time.

When children at Sandy Hook were murdered and the collective response was “dead children are the price you pay for freedom,” the world looked on in horror. In 2017, when a white supremacist neo-Nazi drove his car into a crowd of protesters in Charlottesville, President Trump still couldn't bring himself to say “Nazis are evil.” And the world looked on in horror. The US is averaging a mass shooting almost every day and no one cares. Peoplea are getting gunned down daily in mass shootings in the United states but top news stories are explaining why you shouldn't drink bleach . A few years ago we laughed about the Idiocracy and now we cry about it.

In 2020 during the COVID-19 pandemic, we currently have over 50,000 dead Americans and various state governments, given a complete lack of direction from the federal government, are trying to institute social distancing and quarantines with limited success. Then we see armed white men protesting outside of state capitals because they can't visit Starbucks and they're laughing like they're at a family barbecue. Americans are dying, people are panicking, and armed men show up at state capitals to intimidate the government. Who thought this was a good idea?

And how did we get this broken?

It's watching those men brandishing their weapons, reminding us that we “have a right to bear arms”, that I finally had the core problem with America sink home.

Here in Europe, we have a lot of discussion about our rights and responsibilities as citizens.

In the US, we have a lot of discussion about our rights.

The US is fundamentally a selfish nation.

Libertarians

Now you might be offended by the suggestions that the US is a selfish nation, but Ayn Rand had a huge influence on US political thought and in 1964, she published The Virtue of Selfishness , arguing that it's OK to be “in it for yourself.” It gives a thin veneer of intellectualism to the “fuck you, I got mine” attitude that is so rampant in America today.

Rand wrote her book to help explain her philosophy of Objectivism and Libertarians, while strenously denying being Objectivists (Objectivism touches on many philosophical areas that Libertarians ignore), nonetheless have selfish down to a science. And Libertarians comprise between 10% to 20% of the US electorate , depending on exactly how you choose your definitions. × As for this definition of 'Libertarian', I'm using the right-Libertarian models so prevalent in the US. In particular, the Minarchists and the anarcho-Capitalists.

And even those who deny being Libertarians are, of course, hugely influenced by the US ideals of limited government and individual liberty. And honestly, many Americans really like Libertarians. For example, Ron Paul was adored by many for his constant presidential runs, despite the virulently racist newsletters he published , including such thoughtful material as:

Order was only restored in L.A. [after the Rodney King riots] when it came time for the blacks to pick up their welfare checks.

Or the lovely:

Given the inefficiencies of what DC laughingly calls the criminal justice system, I think we can safely assume that 95 percent of the black males in that city are semi-criminal or entirely criminal.

Of course, Paul's writing was just another example of a well-known phenomenon: Libertarians are overwhelmingly white males from wealthy countries and are their members are frequently racist . × There are many who defend Paul, claiming that he wasn't the author of those particular passages. Except that these newsletters were published for years. Either Paul knew and approved of the racist content, or he was so incompetent that he couldn't even run a newsletter, much less a country. And Ron Paul's son, Senator Rand Paul, who also has a history suggesting he may not be entirely unprejudiced, simply doesn't want to answer any questions about those newsletters .

And at the heart of the matter is when Ron Paul said he would not have voted for the 1964 Civil Rights Act and his son, Rand Paul, kept refusing to answer questions about that, too.

If you're unfamiliar with the 1964 Civil Rights Act , amongst other things it ended the legal practice of segregation and put it place laws to ban many forms of discrimination, including racial. Today, most Americans accept it as a given that racial discrimination should be outlawed. But not all Americans. And it's not even that those Americans are necessarily racist, but they have similar views as Rand Paul :

“I’m not in favor of any discrimination of any form. I would never belong to any club that excluded anybody for race,” Paul said on Maddow’s program. But when pressed on whether he supported aspects of what the Civil Rights law did, including desegregating lunch counters in privately owned restaurants, Paul gave a dodge-y answer: “Does the owner of the restaurant own his restaurant? Or does the government own his restaurant? These are important philosophical debates but not a very practical discussion.”

In other words, Paul feels we have rights, but doesn't give much thought to our responsibilities. He and Libertarians like him are part of the disease infecting America. Their “me, me, me" attitude is destroying the US. And the Republican party is largely in line with the Libertarians, just not as extreme.

More to the point, we can read the Libertarian Party Platform to understand just how much the Libertarian philosophy loves rights and denies responsibilities. You can read it for yourself. It's insane, but Overton Window has shifted so far to the right since the days of Reagan that previously solid Republicans would not be electable in today's environment.

For example, in the early 70s, Republican and Democrat politicians fought to present themselves as environmentalists . The Equal Rights Amendment has wide bi-partison support . And President Nixon, while never publicly calling for a total ban on handguns, nonetheless supported such a ban . In fact, in 1967, California Governor Ronald Reagan, with the full support of the NRA, passed the Mulford Act, a sweeping restriction of gun rights . (Though to be fair, the only reason this was passed was because white politicians realized that black people had guns too.)

So why spend so much time discussing Libertarians? Because they're everywhere. The Cato Institute, a well-known Libertarian think tank, shows that between 7% to 22% of Americans identify as Libertarian, though the wide spread depends greatly on how you identify Libertarians.

This is important because even those who don't identify as Libertarian are more likely to see these views as socially acceptable simply because they're so prevalent. This is due to a cognitive bias called the illusory truth effect , a well-known cognitive bias which makes us more likely to believe in an idea if we've heard it before. Or just consider the famous Joseph Goebbels quote on the big lie :

“If you tell a lie big enough and keep repeating it, people will eventually come to believe it. The lie can be maintained only for such time as the State can shield the people from the political, economic and/or military consequences of the lie. It thus becomes vitally important for the State to use all of its powers to repress dissent, for the truth is the mortal enemy of the lie, and thus by extension, the truth is the greatest enemy of the State.”

Sadly, this works. I get to see this first hand all the time. I was recently speaking to a deeply conservative relative in the US and the discussion turned to health. He mentioned something about how terrible the wait times for medical care must be in France. He had bought in to the “big lie” that medical care in Europe is terrible.

This same relative, back in 2006, upon learning I was moving from the US to work for a company in the UK, mentioned that they must be getting me pretty cheap. After all, immigrants work for less and take jobs from natives. Again, the “big lie” at work.

Don't get me wrong, they're a great person and you'd probably like them if you got to know them, but we don't discuss politics. And that's a shame because the US has gotten divided in terms of left versus right that I hear more and more of my friends lamenting that they've lost friends over this, or they have relatives they can't talk to any more over this. All because the right-wing in America demands their rights while denying their responsibilities.

Or as one of my Libertarian childhood friends now says, “to me, individual freedom always takes precedent over any perceived responsibility to other people.”

And that attitude, right there, is why America is falling apart. It's nothing more than pure, adolescent selfishness.

It's the three-year old child screaming “me, me, me.”

It's the right-wing “fuck you, I got mine” attitude of so much of America.

It's the redneck philosopher, driving down roads paid for with public dollars, enjoying an economy built on the strength of public education, to his farm in the middle of nowhere but with electricity provided via the publicly funded Rural Electrification Act, fondling his rifle and wiping his mouth with the flag while proclaiming “Ahm uh self-made man and I don't own nobody nuthin'.”

That's why America has fallen so far. They've taken the easy road. They've taken the lazy, selfish way out and act as if it's a virtue.
